Scavengr

Project

​Scavengr is a mobile gaming app designed with the goal of connecting gamers with the outdoors by providing a unique and exciting scavenger hunt experience.

Year

2018-2019

UX Methods

User Research, Mental Models, User Flows, Card Sorting, Wireframing & Prototyping, Usability Testing

Tools

Realtime Board, Balsamiq, Lookback, Adobe XD

SURVEYS & USER INTERVIEWS

By distributing an online survey and conducting three user interviews on volunteers in the target audience, a strong understanding of our target audience's needs, motivations and pain points was discovered.

Some key findings and insights include:
  • An overwhelming majority of participants have moved to a new city at least once
  • Participants feel they do not get as many opportunities to make new friends as they would like
  • Most participants travel for pleasure regularly, but only 12% of travelers are interested in tourist hot-spots
  • When traveling, all participants agree that they are more active and spontaneous
  • Most participants look at games as a social experience and would not join a scavenger hunt alone

PERSONAS

After reviewing the results of the survey and interview notes, three distinct personas were developed to capture the prevailing traits of our target audience

Caitriona (The Millennial) is a new grad working in an advertising agency in a new city. She is looking to explore her new city and meet new people.

Leanne (The Traveler) is a working professional who travels a few times each year with her husband. She spends as much time outside as possible and is looking for ways to stay active when they travel.

Michael (The Gamer) spends his evenings and weekends playing online games with friends, but wishes he could spend more time outdoors while still playing games. He is competitive and views games as a way to bond with his friend group.

PRIMARY PERSONAS

Leanne, Caitriona and Michael were kept in mind throughout the design process

USABILITY TESTING

Remote and in person usability tests we conducted with 6 participants to gain insights on the usability and initial UI of Scavengr.

Participants had two direct tasks: search and sign up for a game, and complete a task in a live game, which they completed in a clickable prototype.

A full report including the test script, findings and an updated prototype can be viewed here.

After conducting the tests, a rainbow analysis was a useful tool in identifying errors, negative and positive quotes. By being able to visualize the errors and how frequently they occured, a more informed desicion could be made about what to tackle in the first round of iterations.

IDENTIFYING NEEDS & ITERATING

CREATING THE RIGHT IMPRESSION

After analyzing the test results a plan was developed for the next iteration of Scavengr based on the errors and severity.

The first change came to the home page. When asked for their initial impressions before completing their tasks, all users thought this was a social media app. This is a critical observation and it was clear that the home page needed to be updated to properly portray that Scavengr is first and foremost an app to participate in scavenger hunts.

Key changes include having the games on the home page so it is the first thing users see. The social media aspect has been moved to a secondary page under the Newsfeed menu item.

The second round of iterations refined the design using a grid system, enhanced legibility and updated UI to follow the new style guide.

FOCUS ON ACCESSIBILITY

The second area of focus for the new iteration is in regards to accessibility. One of the test volunteers happened to have a mild form of colour blindness that is common in men.

Throughout the test, many of his comments and observations pertained to the fact that he had difficulty seeing the text on top of the coloured buttons.

It was very lucky that this participant was chosen as the initial UI of the app did not take into account accessibility for those with colour blindness. The decision was made to change the colour scheme of Scavengr to ensure all elements maintain a high level of contrast.

Before: low contrast and a busy colour palette made it difficult to read and gather important information
After: a darker colour palette and larger buttons were chosen to create a more inclusive design

REFINING THE DESIGN

The UI of Scavengr has come a long way from the initial prototype used during usability testing and thanks to the feedback of users it now has a more consistent feel as users move through the app.

A good UI elevates the UX of a system, and that the two must work hand in hand to provide the best experience possible for our users. Through Scavengr's iterations, and the implementation of a style guide and design language, the UI has gone through some significant changes.
